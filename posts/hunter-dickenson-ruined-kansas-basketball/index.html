Did Hunter Dickinson Ruin Kanasas Basketball

After the 2022-2023 Men’s College Basketball Season came to a close, the biggest name in the new transfer portal wasn’t a surprise. Hunter Dickinson, the 7-foot-1 All-American center from Michigan, had two years of eligibility left and a decision that would change the landscape of college basketball forever.

Coming off an unusual early exit in the NCAA Tournament, a Round of 32 loss to Arkansas, Kansas head coach Bill Self knew he had to change something. The Jayhawks had returning talent, upcoming stars coming in, but they needed a big man to secure the paint. A player who has dominated inside on big stages and can replicate the greats who have come before him.

The Dickinson Era…

With an outrageous NIL valuation of $800,000, every major program in the country made their pitch. Kentucky, Maryland, Villanova, and Georgetown were all in the race, but Kansas had something the others didn’t. The track record of developing big men into national champions by the 17-time Big 12 Champion head coach, Bill Self.

For Self, this was more than just another roster spot, it was a statement to the rest of the nation that the Jayhawks are back. After years of relying on guard-heavy lineups, Dickinson could be the centerpiece of the offense and a game changer in the Big 12.

Allen Fieldhouse had new life from the moment the season tipped off, with a new face leading the troops. Backed by a program built on legacy, tradition, and excellence, the Jayhaws ranked No. 1 in the AP Preseason Poll. Could Dickinson be the answered prayer of Lawrence?

While Kansas’s Simple System Rating dropped, Michigan found a new life with Dickinson’s departure. With an already established resume, Hunter found ways to make an impact on the star-studded roster in Lawrence.

Transitioning from the Big 10 to the Big 12 might not seem like a massive difference, but the style and competition level tell a different story. Both conferences are known for their physicality and depth, yet Kansas and Michigan operated in very different systems. So where do these two programs stack up against the rest of the Power 4? Did Dickinson’s arrival ruin the Jayhawks’ established legacy, or did Kansas simply cool off after years of dominance?

Seeing that both programs ranked among the top of all major programs, was Dickinson really that bad for the Jayhawks? Although neither led the nation statistically, both Kansas and Michigan found ways to return to the NCAA tournament and put together statistically solid seasons.

Looking across the Power Four, Kansas ranked near the top of total assists, a reflection of Self’s disciplined offensive system. Their points and rebounds per game stayed consistent with other powerhouse programs, but their true shooting percentage stood out among the best. Meanwhile, Michigan, after losing Dickinson, remained competitive, finishing in the upper half of all of these categories.

So what was the real reason behind the $800,000 transfer? Was Hunter Dickinson the problem, or was it simply the roster Bill Self built around him?

With the 7-footers’ overall shooting percentages decreasing, he found other ways to make an impact on this roster while rebounding and finding other stars on his team. With a heavy load for the 5-star transfer to fill and a lot of hype built on short success, was Lawrence the best home for Dickinson?

In the end, Hunter Dickinson’s time at Kansas will be remembered as a mix of promise and what could have been. He brought leadership, experience, and attention back to the post, qualities any program would value, but the results didn’t quite match the hype. The Jayhawks have been competitive but have not reached their full potential since their historic 2022 championship.

This comes to ask, did Dickinson really ruin Kansas basketball, or was Bill Self just another coach navigating the challenges of the transfer portal? For the upcoming season, Bill Self invested in both top freshmen and transfers, rounding out a roster valued at nearly $10 million. But is bringing in fifth-year seniors truly worth the cost and time, or would it be more beneficial to focus on developing young freshmen with untapped potential?
